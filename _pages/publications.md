My research spans two interconnected fields: colloidal and surface chemistry, and chemistry education. My earlier work focused on the physical chemistry of surfaces — developing durable superhydrophobic coatings, investigating lubrication and slip at nanoscale liquid interfaces using atomic force microscopy, and probing the structural forces that govern interactions between charged interfaces in nanoparticle suspensions and polyelectrolyte solutions. These investigations were carried out during my PhD at the University of Sydney and subsequent postdoctoral positions at the University of Geneva and the University of Bristol. Since returning to Australia and joining UNSW College in 2024, my research focus has shifted toward chemistry education, where I apply the same rigorous, evidence-based approach to understanding how students learn. My current work examines the role of generative AI in scaffolding peer learning, the impact of multimodal feedback on student engagement and reflection, and — as Principal Investigator — how offering students choice among AI-generated pre-lab modalities (grounded in Universal Design for Learning principles) shapes their preparation, confidence, and performance in first-year chemistry laboratories.




## Chemical Education

### Think–Pair–Chatbot–Share: AI-Facilitated Peer Learning in Chemistry
**Naeyaert, P.J.P., Scarratt, L.R.J., Murphy, T., & Pullen, R.** (2025)  
*Journal of Chemical Education*, 102(9), 3936–3944.  
[https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jchemed.5c00438](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jchemed.5c00438){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** This study introduces and evaluates the Think–Pair–Chatbot–Share (TPCS) model, an adaptation of the traditional Think–Pair–Share (TPS) framework where a generative AI chatbot is integrated to scaffold learning and provide feedback. First-year chemistry students learning ionization energy and atomic structure using traditional TPS were compared to those using the Gen-AI-facilitated TPCS model. For students who completed all stages, the TPCS group recorded a higher proportion of correct share stage responses in three of the four tasks. However, the TPCS intervention was associated with lower overall participation, suggesting potential challenges related to sustained engagement. These findings offer the first classroom-level evidence of AI-mediated TPS in action within tertiary chemistry education.

### Enhancing Student Learning Through Video Feedback and Structured Reflection
**Murphy, T., Scarratt, L., Black, J., Naeyaert, P., & Matovu, H.** (2025)  
*Proceedings of The Australian Conference on Science and Mathematics Education 2025*.

**Abstract:** This study examines student perceptions of video feedback compared to written feedback in relation to relevance, clarity, helpfulness, and emotional response. Students who received video feedback reported significantly higher levels of positive emotion after viewing the feedback compared to those who received written feedback. Higher ratings for relevance, clarity, and helpfulness indicate that students may find video feedback more accessible and actionable. The findings have practical implications for educators seeking to enhance the overall feedback experience for students in science and mathematics education.

## Surface Chemistry & Colloidal Science

### Drop Friction and Failure on Superhydrophobic and Slippery Surfaces
**Naga, A., Scarratt, L.R.J., Neto, C., Papadopoulos, P., & Vollmer, D.** (2025)  
*ACS Nano*, 19(20), 18902–18928.  
[https://doi.org/10.1021/acsnano.5c01142](https://doi.org/10.1021/acsnano.5c01142){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** The mobility of drops on a surface influences how much water and energy is required to clean the surface. Superhydrophobic and slippery liquid-infused surfaces both display high drop mobility despite being lubricated by fluids having very different viscosities. Superhydrophobic surfaces rely on micro- and/or nanoscale textures to trap air pockets beneath drops, minimising solid–liquid contact. On liquid-infused surfaces, these solid textures are filled with an immiscible liquid lubricant. This review describes the criteria needed to obtain stable wetting states with low drop friction and high mobility on both surfaces, and discusses the mechanisms that explain the origins of friction on each surface. Drops can collapse from the low-friction Cassie state to the high-friction Wenzel state on both surfaces, but the transition follows different pathways. On liquid-infused surfaces, the wetting ridge near the drop edge plays a central role in triggering collapse, a phenomenon not observed on superhydrophobic surfaces.

### Forces Between Interfaces in Concentrated Nanoparticle Suspensions and Polyelectrolyte Solutions
**Scarratt, L.R.J., Trefalt, G., & Borkovec, M.** (2021)  
*Current Opinion in Colloid & Interface Science*, 55, 101482.  
[https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cocis.2021.101482](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cocis.2021.101482){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** This article provides an overview of interactions between charged interfaces across concentrated suspensions of charged nanoparticles or solutions of polyelectrolytes. These systems bear many similarities, and the like-charged and oppositely charged situations are distinguished. In the like-charged case, oscillatory structural forces dominate at larger separations, while double layer forces are relevant at smaller ones. The parameters entering the model show good agreement with independently measured quantities. In the oppositely charged case, attractive interactions dominate, leading to surface charge neutralisation and overcharging. The article covers recent advances in Debye–Hückel and Poisson–Boltzmann approaches, free energy profiles, parameters and scaling laws, and the like-charged versus oppositely charged scenarios.

### Thickness of the Particle-Free Layer Near Charged Interfaces in Suspensions of Like-Charged Nanoparticles
**Kosior, D., Gvaramia, M., Scarratt, L.R.J., Maroni, P., Trefalt, G., & Borkovec, M.** (2021)  
*Soft Matter*, 17(25), 6212–6224.  
[https://doi.org/10.1039/D1SM00584G](https://doi.org/10.1039/D1SM00584G){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** When a suspension of charged nanoparticles is in contact with a like-charged water–solid interface, a particle-free layer forms next to that interface. This study provides reliable measurements of the thickness of this particle-free layer using three different techniques: optical reflectivity, atomic force microscopy, and QCM. The characteristic separation between two interfaces observed in direct force measurements corresponds to twice the layer thickness for an isolated interface. These results are rationalised within the Poisson–Boltzmann framework by treating the nanoparticles as highly charged co-ions.

### Structural and Double Layer Forces Between Silica Surfaces in Suspensions of Negatively Charged Nanoparticles
**Scarratt, L.R.J., Kubiak, K., Maroni, P., Trefalt, G., & Borkovec, M.** (2020)  
*Langmuir*, 36(47), 14443–14452.  
[https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.langmuir.0c02289](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.langmuir.0c02289){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** Direct force measurements between negatively charged silica microparticles were carried out in suspensions of like-charged nanoparticles using atomic force microscopy. In agreement with previous studies, oscillatory force profiles are observed at larger separation distances. At smaller distances, soft and strongly repulsive forces are present, caused by double layer repulsion between the like-charged surfaces, which can be quantitatively interpreted with the Poisson–Boltzmann (PB) model adapted to a strongly asymmetric electrolyte. The nanoparticles are modelled as highly charged co-ions, while the counter ions are monovalent. This model permits extraction of the effective charge of the nanoparticles and also explains the presence of a particle-free layer close to the interface.

### Structuring of Colloidal Silica Nanoparticle Suspensions Near Water–Silica Interfaces Probed by Specular Neutron Reflectivity
**Maroni, P., Gvaramia, M., Kosior, D., Kubiak, K., Scarratt, L., Smith, A.M., et al.** (2020)  
*Physical Chemistry Chemical Physics*, 22(11), 6449–6456.  
[https://doi.org/10.1039/D0CP00465K](https://doi.org/10.1039/D0CP00465K){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** Structuring of aqueous suspensions of colloidal silica nanoparticles near an isolated planar silica–water interface is studied by specular neutron reflectivity. The reflectivity data clearly show that the suspensions develop a damped, oscillatory concentration profile in the direction normal to the interface. The wavelengths of these oscillations agree well with those independently determined by direct force measurements in the slit geometry. The reflectivity data further demonstrate that the oscillatory structure persists over several layers and that the first particle layer is separated from the interface by a particle-free region.

### Large Effective Slip on Lubricated Surfaces Measured with Colloidal Probe AFM
**Scarratt, L.R.J., Zhu, L., & Neto, C.** (2020)  
*Langmuir*, 36(21), 6033–6040.  
[https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.langmuir.9b02935](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.langmuir.9b02935){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** In this work, the interfacial boundary conditions at the interface between two immiscible liquids under laminar flow are studied. Hydrodynamic drainage forces acting on a colloid probe approaching a flat Teflon film coated with silicone oil films, submerged in a sucrose solution, are measured using atomic force microscopy. On Teflon substrates, silicone oil films of thickness several hundred nanometers could be stabilised, and the effective slip length over these was found to be of the order of several hundred nanometers, increasing with increasing silicone oil film thickness. The fitted slip length values weakly increased with increasing shear rates. The high values of effective slip length indicate that lubricant-infused surfaces are likely to reduce drag on length scales that approach the macroscopic scales.

### How Slippery Are SLIPS? Measuring Effective Slip on Lubricated Surfaces with Colloidal Probe Atomic Force Microscopy
**Scarratt, L.R.J., Zhu, L., & Neto, C.** (2019)  
*Langmuir*, 35(8), 2976–2982.  
[https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.langmuir.8b03767](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.langmuir.8b03767){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** Lubricant-infused surfaces have attracted great attention and are described as slippery liquid-infused porous surfaces (SLIPS). The hydrodynamic drainage forces on SLIPS were measured by colloidal probe atomic force microscopy and the effective slip length quantified over a nanothin silicone oil layer on hydrophobised (OTS-coated) silicon wafers. The thickness of a stable silicone oil film on OTS–Si under sucrose solution was determined to be 1.8 ± 1.3 nm, inducing an average effective slip length of 29 ± 3 nm — very close to that of an uninfused OTS substrate. Both nano- and macroscale results reflect the immobilised nature of a silicone oil layer of thickness around 2 nm within an underlying monolayer, with important implications for the design of drag-reducing coatings using lubricant infusion.

### Marine Antifouling Behavior of Lubricant-Infused Nanowrinkled Polymeric Surfaces
**Ware, C.S., Smith-Palmer, T., Peppou-Chapman, S., Scarratt, L.R.J., et al.** (2018)  
*ACS Applied Materials & Interfaces*, 10(4), 4173–4182.  
[https://doi.org/10.1021/acsami.7b14736](https://doi.org/10.1021/acsami.7b14736){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** A new family of polymeric, lubricant-infused, nanostructured wrinkled surfaces was designed that effectively retains inert nontoxic silicone oil after draining by spin-coating and vigorous shear for 2 weeks. The wrinkled surfaces were fabricated using three different polymers (Teflon AF, polystyrene, and poly(4-vinylpyridine)) and two shrinkable substrates, with Teflon on Polyshrink found to be the most effective system. The infused surfaces displayed stability in seawater and inhibited growth of *Pseudoalteromonas spp.* bacteria up to 99%. Field tests in the waters of Sydney Harbour over 7 weeks showed that silicone oil infusion inhibited the attachment of algae. The infused wrinkled surfaces have high transparency and are mouldable, making them suited to protect the windows of underwater sensors and cameras.

### A Review on the Mechanical and Thermodynamic Robustness of Superhydrophobic Surfaces
**Scarratt, L.R.J., Steiner, U., & Neto, C.** (2017)  
*Advances in Colloid and Interface Science*, 246, 133–152.  
[https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cis.2017.05.018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cis.2017.05.018){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** Advancements in the fabrication and study of superhydrophobic surfaces have been significant over the past decade, and the study of special wettability surfaces can be considered mainstream. While the fabrication of superhydrophobic surfaces is well-understood, their widespread application is hampered by their lack of robustness. This review covers recent progress in understanding and improving both the mechanical robustness (resistance to wear, impact, and abrasion) and the thermodynamic robustness (resistance to wetting transitions) of superhydrophobic surfaces, and discusses strategies toward producing durable surfaces for real-world applications.

### Durable Superhydrophobic Surfaces via Spontaneous Wrinkling of Teflon AF
**Scarratt, L.R.J., Hoatson, B.S., Wood, E.S., Hawkett, B.S., & Neto, C.** (2016)  
*ACS Applied Materials & Interfaces*, 8(10), 6743–6750.  
[https://doi.org/10.1021/acsami.5b12165](https://doi.org/10.1021/acsami.5b12165){:target="_blank"}

**Abstract:** The fabrication of both single-scale and hierarchical superhydrophobic surfaces is reported, created by exploiting the spontaneous wrinkling of a rigid Teflon AF film on two types of shrinkable plastic substrates. Sub-100 nm to micrometric wrinkles were reproducibly generated with remarkable control over size and hierarchy. Hierarchical Teflon AF wrinkled surfaces showed extremely high water repellence (contact angle 172°) and very low contact angle hysteresis (2°), resulting in droplets rolling off at tilt angles lower than 5°. The wrinkling process intimately binds the Teflon AF layer with its substrate, making these surfaces mechanically robust. The fabrication procedure is extremely rapid, requiring only 2 min of thermal annealing, and uses inexpensive materials.
